Chips & Compute

Nvidia

Nvidia is the dominant supplier of AI accelerators, founded in 1993 by Jensen Huang, Chris Malachowsky, and Curtis Priem, and headquartered in Santa Clara, California. Originally a PC graphics company (the GeForce line), it pivoted GPUs into general-purpose compute with CUDA in 2006 and is now the most valuable semiconductor company in the world, having crossed multi-trillion-dollar market capitalization. Its flagship data-center products are the Hopper generation (H100, H200) and the Blackwell generation (B100/B200 and the GB200 Grace Blackwell superchip), sold inside DGX systems and the rack-scale GB200 NVL72. Nvidia is fabless, manufacturing on TSMC and packaging with CoWoS, and pairs its silicon with NVLink/NVSwitch interconnect, ConnectX/BlueField networking from its Mellanox acquisition, and Spectrum-X/Quantum InfiniBand fabrics.

strategyStrategy

Nvidia sells full-stack AI infrastructure rather than bare chips: GPUs, NVLink/InfiniBand networking, DGX and HGX systems, and a deep software moat in CUDA, cuDNN, TensorRT, Triton, and the enterprise AI Enterprise suite. It monetizes through hyperscalers (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta), neoclouds (CoreWeave, Lambda), enterprises, and sovereign-AI national programs, capturing the lion's share of training and a large share of inference spend. The company deliberately keeps annual cadence high to stay ahead of buyers' depreciation cycles and competitors. It positions itself as the platform for accelerated computing broadly, not just AI, spanning Omniverse, autonomous vehicles (DRIVE), and robotics (Jetson/Isaac).

auto_awesomeWhat Sets Them Apart

The genuine moat is CUDA plus the surrounding library ecosystem accumulated over nearly two decades, which makes porting workloads to rivals expensive and risky. Nvidia uniquely ships rack-scale systems where NVLink turns 72 Blackwell GPUs into a single coherent compute domain (GB200 NVL72), an interconnect advantage competitors using PCIe or Ethernet cannot easily match. Owning Mellanox gives it the networking layer that determines real cluster throughput, so it sells the whole datacenter fabric, not just the compute node. Its software releases continually extract more performance from installed hardware, lengthening the value of each generation.

routeRoadmap

Nvidia has committed to a roughly annual product cadence: Blackwell Ultra (GB300) followed by the Rubin architecture and Vera CPU, with Rubin Ultra and the Feynman generation signposted further out. It is pushing rack-scale and even multi-rack coherent designs, co-packaged optics to scale interconnect bandwidth, and continued investment in inference-optimized variants as the workload mix shifts from training to serving. Strategic bets include sovereign AI deployments, enterprise agentic AI, physical AI/robotics via Isaac and Omniverse, and automotive. It is also deepening networking with Spectrum-X Ethernet to court customers who resist InfiniBand.

lightbulbWhy This Approach

Nvidia's thesis is that AI is a new computing platform and that whoever owns the developer ecosystem and the full system stack captures the value, not whoever has the fastest single chip. It chose general-purpose programmable GPUs plus CUDA over fixed-function ASICs because flexibility let it ride every wave from gaming to crypto to deep learning to transformers without redesigning silicon for each. It invested early and heavily in software when that looked unprofitable, betting that lock-in and performance-per-watt at the rack level would compound. Buying Mellanox reflected the conviction that at scale the network, not the GPU, becomes the bottleneck.
