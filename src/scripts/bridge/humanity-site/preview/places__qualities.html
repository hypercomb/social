Places · What makes a centre

The texture of a place built for being human.

Rooms to stay the night. Space to gather in many sizes. A table to share, a garden to tend, a quiet corner to think. The qualities of a centre are simple — and they matter more than they look.

What turns a building into a place of belonging isn’t grandeur — it’s thoughtfulness. Enough beds that people can stay long enough to soften. Rooms that fit a pair, a circle, and a crowd. A kitchen that can feed everyone at once. And the small touches — a labyrinth, a library, a garden — that invite you to slow down.

The qualities we look for

Room specifications

Places to stay overnight — so a retreat can last more than an afternoon, and people can arrive from far away.

Numbers of participants

Spaces that fit a quiet dyad, a circle of a dozen, and a shared meal for the whole room.

Other facilities

The gentle extras — a garden, a labyrinth, a library, a fire — that make a place worth returning to.

What a mature centre can look like

For a sense of scale, take the independent Bethlehem Centre in Nanaimo — an established retreat centre we admire (and are not affiliated with). By its own account:

4
guesthouses, with around 37 rooms to stay in
7
distinct gathering venues for circles big and small
~50
seats at the table for a shared meal
1
Chartres-style labyrinth to walk inward, plus a garden, library, and reflective pond

Figures above describe Bethlehem Centre, quoted from its own site — an illustration of the form, not a claim about our own facilities. Most Humanity Centres will start smaller and grow into themselves.
