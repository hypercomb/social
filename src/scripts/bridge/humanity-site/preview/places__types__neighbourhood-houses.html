Places · Neighbourhood houses

A community living room on your own street.

The neighbourhood-house tradition is more than a century old: place-based, multi-service hubs where people connect, learn, and belong. We’re learning from it — humbly, and in our own words.

The neighbourhood-house movement describes each house as a “community living room — a place where everyone belongs,” caring for the whole person, the whole family, the whole neighbourhood, and the whole community. That phrase, and that century of practice, belong to the movement — not to us. We name them with respect, as the tradition a Humanity Centre stands in.

What we take from it is an ethos: a neighbourhood house isn’t there to be the solution for people — it’s there to offer the room, the relationships, and the support so a community can grow its own way forward. Belonging first; everything else follows.

The ethos we share

Everyone belongs

A door open to the whole neighbourhood — every age, background, and belief, no membership required.

The whole person

Meeting people as whole humans in whole families and neighbourhoods, not as a single need to be processed.

Empowerment, not rescue

Offering tools, knowledge, and real support — helping people find their own way, not being the answer for them.

Rooted in place

Shaped by the actual street it sits on, responding to what this community actually needs.

A place to take off your coat, sit down, and be met — somewhere you belong before you’re asked to be anything.
In the spirit of the neighbourhood-house tradition

Humanity Centres in British Columbia gather on the traditional, ancestral, and unceded territories of the Coast Salish peoples — including the Snuneymuxw (Nanaimo); the Semiahmoo, Katzie, and Kwantlen (Surrey); and the Musqueam, Squamish, and Tsleil-Waututh (Vancouver / South False Creek) — who have cared for these lands since time immemorial. We hold these relationships with gratitude and responsibility, as the first relationship we tend.
